## Clinical history

Patient A is a 70-year-old individual with a 14-year history of seropositive
rheumatoid arthritis (RA). Disease was initially controlled with hydroxychloroquine
monotherapy for approximately three years; progression led to the addition of
methotrexate (MTX) at 20 mg/week. After 18 months of combination therapy, the
patient developed MTX-associated hepatotoxicity (ALT >3x ULN confirmed on two
successive labs six weeks apart), necessitating MTX discontinuation.

A trial of leflunomide (20 mg/day for 12 weeks) was initiated as an alternative
conventional DMARD but was discontinued after the patient developed persistent
peripheral neuropathy attributed to the agent, documented in the treating
rheumatologist's notes. Sulfasalazine was not attempted; the patient has a
documented sulfa allergy (urticaria) that constitutes a medical contraindication.

Current disease burden: 12 tender joints, 9 swollen joints; DAS28-CRP 5.6
(moderate-to-high disease activity); HAQ-DI 1.4. Latent TB screening (IGRA)
performed: negative. Hepatitis B surface antigen: negative; anti-HBs: positive.
Hepatitis C antibody: negative. No active infection. No prior malignancy. Cardiac
function normal per most recent echocardiogram.

## Clinical justification

The treating rheumatologist has determined that Patient A meets criteria for biologic
DMARD therapy. Two conventional DMARD courses have been trialed:

1. Methotrexate 20 mg/week — discontinued due to hepatotoxicity (elevated
   transaminases), documented by serial laboratory results.
2. Leflunomide 20 mg/day — discontinued due to peripheral neuropathy, documented in
   office notes.

A third conventional option (sulfasalazine) is contraindicated due to sulfa allergy.

Adalimumab is an FDA-approved TNF inhibitor for moderate-to-severe RA in adults and
is listed on the SilverScript Part D formulary (S5810-001) as a Tier 5 Specialty drug
subject to prior authorization and step-therapy requirements. The step-therapy
requirement is satisfied by the two documented DMARD failures, and a step-therapy
exception is warranted for sulfasalazine given the documented allergy.

The clinical evidence base — including the FDA-approved prescribing information and
CMS Part D formulary entry — supports approval of this prior-authorization request.
The patient has no contraindications to adalimumab identified in the FDA label.
