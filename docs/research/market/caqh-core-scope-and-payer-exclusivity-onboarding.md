# CAQH CORE Scope and Payer Exclusivity — Onboarding Implications

## 2026-05-15 — Does CAQH CORE create an affirmative obligation to route all claim status and remittance through certified clearinghouses, or is cliqueue's on-chain settlement layer categorically outside CORE's scope?

- **CAQH CORE does not mandate clearinghouse usage.** The operating rules explicitly state: "CAQH CORE Operating Rules do not require a 'direct' connect… the Phase I CAQH CORE Rules do not require any specific architecture." Health plans may route HIPAA standard transactions through clearinghouses or handle them directly; CORE governs capabilities and data standards, not infrastructure topology. Source: https://www.caqh.org/faq-page/511

- **CAQH CORE certification is voluntary.** CMS does not require compliance with CORE certification. CORE administers a voluntary certification process for a fee. The ACA § 1104 mandate applies to HIPAA-covered standard transactions (837, 835, 276/277, 834, 270/271) and requires covered entities to comply with adopted operating rules — but CORE certification itself is not the legal obligation; HHS rulemaking adopting specific rules is. Sources: https://www.caqh.org/core/operating-rules-mandate, https://www.cms.gov/priorities/key-initiatives/burden-reduction/administrative-simplification/operating-rules/faqs

- **Phase IV CORE 450 (837) explicitly carves out Real-Time Adjudication (RTA).** The Phase IV CAQH CORE 450 Health Care Claim Infrastructure Rule states: its requirements "do not apply to the conduct of a Real Time Adjudication (RTA) process in which a health care claim is submitted to a HIPAA-covered health plan or its agent in Real Time and the HIPAA-covered health plan or its agent adjudicates that claim in Real Time during the same communications session." Cliqueue's on-chain real-time settlement pathway qualifies as RTA and is therefore **outside CORE Phase IV 450's scope**. Source: https://www.caqh.org/sites/default/files/core/phase-iv/450_837-infrastructure-rule.pdf

- **CAQH CORE governs the EDI transaction layer, not supplemental audit/settlement rails.** The operating rules specify data content and format for HIPAA standard transactions (X12 837/835/276/277). An on-chain settlement layer that anchors hashes and settlement amounts is not a HIPAA standard transaction — it is an auxiliary payment and audit system. No CORE operating rule covers blockchain settlement events. CAQH CORE Phase IV RTA guidance explicitly allows payment to be processed after adjudication ("835 delivery is decoupled from the same-session 837 submission"), consistent with cliqueue's off-chain EDI adapter + on-chain settlement architecture. Source: https://www.caqh.org/core/caqh-core-payment-and-remittance-operating-rules

- **Payer clearinghouse exclusivity is contractual, not regulatory.** Anthem/BCBS requires all new submitters to use the Availity EDI Gateway for Anthem EDI transactions. This is a provider contract term, not a CAQH CORE or HIPAA requirement. Major payers (Anthem/BCBS, UHC, Aetna) have exclusive clearinghouse arrangements that govern the 837 claim submission channel. Adding cliqueue's on-chain settlement layer alongside an existing EDI rail does not submit a second 837 — it anchors a hash and a settlement proof after adjudication. This distinction is material for legal onboarding review: the question for hospital counsel is whether the provider contract prohibits *any* alternative electronic routing of claim-derived data, not merely 837 resubmission. Sources: https://www.anthem.com/provider/individual-commercial/edi, https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/availity-platform-payers-analysis

- **The 36% payer exclusivity exposure (from prior research) applies to the 837 EDI submission channel.** The AMA survey finding that 36% of providers face payer-imposed contract exclusivity clauses (documented in the Change Healthcare concentration research) specifically identifies providers who cannot route 837 EDI claims through alternative clearinghouses. Cliqueue's on-chain layer does not replace or duplicate the 837 EDI submission — it is a post-adjudication settlement and audit layer. The contractual risk is narrower than initially framed: it is triggered only if the payer contract includes language prohibiting sharing claim-derived data with any third-party system other than the designated clearinghouse. Source: prior research at docs/research/market/change-healthcare-clearinghouse-concentration-and-decentralization-appetite.md

- **CAQH is developing new 2025–2026 claim status operating rules** focused on standardizing error code combinations and enabling real-time data exchange, with a 2026 market-based review. No indication these address blockchain, decentralized settlement, or non-EDI rails. Source: https://www.caqh.org/core/priority-topics

**Design implication:** Cliqueue's on-chain settlement layer is categorically outside CAQH CORE's regulatory scope — it is not a HIPAA standard transaction and is not subject to CORE operating rules. The material onboarding legal risk is narrow: hospital counsel must review the specific payer contract for any clause prohibiting sharing claim-derived data with third-party systems other than the designated clearinghouse; the risk is contractual, not regulatory. The hospital onboarding checklist should include a payer-contract legal review step scoped specifically to "data sharing with non-clearinghouse third parties post-adjudication," not a broad CORE compliance review.
