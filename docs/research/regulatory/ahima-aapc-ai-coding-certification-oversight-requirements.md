# AHIMA/AAPC Certification Rules and AI-Only Coding Constraints

## 2026-05-16 — How do AHIMA/AAPC certification rules constrain AI-only coding — is there a CCA/CCS/CPC equivalent expected for AI agents, or published guidance?

- **No federal regulation mandates universal certified-coder review of AI-generated codes** as of May 2026. CMS, OIG, and HHS have not issued a rule requiring a CCS, CCA, CPC, or RHIA to sign off every AI-coded claim before submission. This is confirmed by multiple sources including Tucker Ellis LLP (healthcare FCA counsel) and the CodingNetwork.com analysis of regulatory gaps. ([Tucker Ellis: Avoiding FCA Landmines in AI-Assisted Coding](https://www.tuckerellis.com/alerts/avoiding-false-claims-act-landmines-in-ai-assisted-coding-and-medical-billing/); [CodingNetwork: Human Oversight in AI Coding 2025](https://codingnetwork.com/why-human-oversight-in-ai-medical-coding-remains-essential-in-2025/))

- **OIG's February 2026 Medicare Advantage Compliance Program Guidance is the strongest regulatory signal**: it explicitly flags AI-driven diagnosis prompting as a potentially abusive practice and requires that human-in-the-loop review be substantive — coders must be able to cite the specific clinical documentation supporting each accepted code. Rubber-stamp review (e.g., accepting 85% of suggestions at 0.5 seconds/chart) collapses as a legal defense under FCA scienter. This guidance applies to Medicare Advantage risk-adjustment coding — its direct applicability to fee-for-service outpatient claim coding is not stated. ([OIG MA Compliance Program Guidance Feb 2026](https://oig.hhs.gov/compliance/ma-icpg/); [Sidley: OIG Releases Long-Awaited MA Guidance](https://www.sidley.com/en/insights/newsupdates/2026/02/oig-releases-long-awaited-medicare-advantage-compliance-program-guidance))

- **OIG General Compliance Program Guidance (Nov 2023) recommends internal auditing of AI-coded claims** and auditing of medical necessity by "an appropriately credentialed clinician" as part of claims reviews — but this is a compliance program recommendation, not a submission requirement. No claim-level certification is required before filing. ([OIG GCPG 2023](https://oig.hhs.gov/documents/compliance-guidance/1135/HHS-OIG-GCPG-2023.pdf))

- **Humana and Cigna have contractual (not regulatory) credentialed coder attestation requirements**: as of Q2 2025, both payers require providers to attest that AI-generated codes have been validated by credentialed coders before the claim can drop. These are payer contract obligations — a breach triggers contract remedies and potential recoupment, not automatic FCA liability. ([CodingNetwork 2025](https://codingnetwork.com/why-human-oversight-in-ai-medical-coding-remains-essential-in-2025/); [2026 Guide to AI in Medical Coding](https://helpsquad.com/blog/the-2026-guide-to-ai-in-medical-coding/))

- **The inpatient vs. outpatient distinction for human review is a best-practice framework, not a regulatory bright line**: AHIMA's August 2024 Journal of AHIMA opinion piece ("A Framework for Autonomous Coding in the Inpatient Setting") proposes that coders become "validators" in the inpatient setting — but this is an academic opinion piece, not AHIMA policy. No AHIMA or AAPC formal position statement requires certified coder sign-off for outpatient claims processed by AI. The inpatient/outpatient enforcement distinction exists in practice because inpatient DRG codes carry higher dollar values and audit scrutiny. ([Journal of AHIMA: A Framework for Autonomous Coding in the Inpatient Setting](https://journal.ahima.org/page/a-framework-for-autonomous-coding-in-the-inpatient-setting))

- **Medicare Advantage risk-adjustment is the highest-risk autonomous coding domain**: Kaiser Permanente ($556M, Jan 2026), DaVita ($270M, 2018), Cigna ($172M, 2023), and Independent Health/DxID ($100M, 2024) enforcement actions all involve MA risk-adjustment diagnosis inflation — not fee-for-service outpatient ICD-10 coding. The DOJ Working Group specifically targets "vendors and providers [that] use AI to auto-code unsupported diagnoses to achieve higher [MA] payments." Fee-for-service outpatient coding enforcement (UCHealth, $23M, 2024) involved automated CPT upcoding of EM visits — a different pattern. ([Health Law Attorney Blog, March 2026](https://www.healthlawattorneyblog.com/ai-is-billing-your-patients-who-holds-the-bag-the-rise-of-ambient-billing-and-coding-tools-and-the-legal-minefield-your-practice-is-walking-into/))

- **AHIMA's formal posture on AI is advisory, not prescriptive**: AHIMA's AI resource page offers educational frameworks and governance templates but has not issued a binding position statement requiring certified coder review for AI-coded claims. AHIMA's consensus statement (as part of the Health IT End-Users Alliance) calls for AI to "augment, not replace, human expertise" and for "policymakers to establish risk-based regulations" — advocacy framing, not existing compliance obligation. ([AHIMA: Milestone Consensus Statement on AI, 2025](https://www.ahima.org/news-publications/press-room-press-releases/2025-press-releases/milestone-consensus-statement-on-ai-issued-by-power-users-as-part-of-the-health-it-end-users-alliance/))

- **HHS HTI-5 rule (proposed, deregulatory direction)**: ONC's proposed HTI-5 rule moves toward deregulation of clinical AI tools, removing Biden-era "model card" disclosure requirements for CDS algorithms and expanding definitions of "access" and "use" to include AI-driven processes — signaling a federal regulatory posture that expects industry self-governance for AI coding tools rather than prescriptive certification mandates. ([Nelson Mullins: HTI-5 Analysis](https://www.nelsonmullins.com/insights/blogs/ai-task-force/ai/hhs-proposes-rule-to-deregulate-health-it-and-advance-ai-interoperability-hti-5))

**Design implication:** cliqueue's on-chain human attestor (SBT-gated) architecture for inpatient DRG claims correctly addresses the highest-risk domain.
