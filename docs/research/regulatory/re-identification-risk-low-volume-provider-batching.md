# Re-identification Risk: Low-Volume Provider On-Chain Data and Batching Threshold

## 2026-05-15 — What is the minimum claim volume threshold at which a provider's per-claim `(billedCents, adjudicatedCents, timestamp)` tuple on a public chain becomes statistically re-identifiable under §164.514(b)(1) Expert Determination, and should cliqueue implement automatic batching below a configurable `minBatchThreshold`?

- **No universal numerical threshold exists in §164.514(b)(1).** HHS OCR's 2012 Expert Determination guidance explicitly states the Privacy Rule "does not contain an explicit numerical level of identification risk that is deemed to universally meet the 'very small' level." The expert must define an acceptable risk level based on the specific data, context, and the capabilities of anticipated recipients. ([HHS HIPAA De-identification Guidance, 2012](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/special-topics/de-identification/index.html))

- **CMS's operational standard for public payment data is n ≥ 11.** The CMS Physician and Other Supplier Public Use File (PUF) suppresses any aggregate cell derived from 10 or fewer beneficiaries: *"no cell containing a value of 1 to 10 can be reported directly."* This CMS cell-size suppression policy is the closest regulatory analogue to a threshold cliqueue can adopt for per-provider on-chain claim counts. ([ResDAC: CMS Cell Size Suppression Policy](https://resdac.org/articles/cms-cell-size-suppression-policy); [CMS Physician PUF Methodology](https://www.cms.gov/research-statistics-data-and-systems/statistics-trends-and-reports/medicare-provider-charge-data/downloads/medicare-physician-and-other-supplier-puf-methodology.pdf))

- **Population-based k-anonymity research supports a minimum equivalence class of 20 (5% re-identification probability) for public-release data where consequences of re-identification are severe.** A 2020 peer-reviewed protocol (PMC7501321) adopted k=20 as the industry-accepted threshold for datasets where re-identification consequences are "extremely high" — corresponding to a 5% max re-identification probability. For lower-stakes contexts, regulators such as Health Canada recommend a 9% threshold (k≈11). ([PMC7501321: Less than five is less than ideal](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7501321/))

- **Payment/billing amounts are not one of HIPAA's 18 Safe Harbor identifiers but function as quasi-identifiers in combination.** Billing amounts (`billedCents`, `adjudicatedCents`) are not listed among the 18 PHI identifiers requiring Safe Harbor removal, but under Expert Determination they are treated as quasi-identifiers that must be evaluated contextually. For rare or high-cost procedures (e.g., a single $180,000 CABG per quarter from a two-surgeon cardiac center), the combination of amount + timestamp is highly distinctive and may be sufficient for re-identification through cross-reference with publicly available claims data or payer databases. No peer-reviewed study specifically quantifies the re-identification probability for dollar-amount tuples at the provider level, representing a gap in the literature. ([PMC6450246: Assessing and Minimizing Re-identification Risk](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6450246/))

- **Blockchain transaction amount fingerprinting is a documented EVM de-anonymization attack vector.** Ethereum privacy research (Biryukov & Tikhomirov 2020, arXiv 2005.14051, "Blockchain is Watching You") demonstrates that transaction amounts function as quasi-identifiers for address clustering. A variant of the Danaan-gift attack uses distinctive payment amounts to link confidential transaction overlays back to originating addresses. For a public EVM chain like Somnia, where all `ClaimSettled` events with `billedCents` and `adjudicatedCents` are permanently visible, a motivated attacker with access to payer remittance data (835 files) could cross-reference on-chain amounts to link claim events to specific patients, particularly for low-volume specialty providers with distinctive billing patterns. ([arXiv 2005.14051](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14051))

- **The re-identification risk falls disproportionately on small providers, not large ones.** Research consistently shows that re-identification risk is not distributed uniformly — it concentrates on small-cell records. A solo practitioner performing 3 claims/month for a rare condition (e.g., pediatric epilepsy surgery) presents a fundamentally different risk profile than a 500-bed hospital processing 10,000 outpatient claims/month. The HHS 2012 guidance explicitly notes that *"the risk of identification determined for one particular set of data in the context of a specific environment may not be appropriate for the same set of data in a different environment."* Per-provider expert determinations (or dynamic batching calibrated per provider) are the conservative approach. ([HHS De-identification Guidance](https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/ocr/privacy/hipaa/understanding/coveredentities/De-identification/hhs_deid_guidance.pdf))

- **Expert Determination scope is context-specific: a single network-level determination does not automatically cover all hospital deployments.** HHS guidance states the determination must reference the "anticipated recipient" and the specific context. A cliqueue network-wide expert determination report could cover the on-chain data model if it explicitly characterizes the full range of deployment contexts — including low-volume providers — and concludes that the risk remains "very small" across that range. However, if a rural single-specialty practice deploys cliqueue and its claim volume falls below the modeled lower bound, that practice would fall outside the determination's scope and require supplementary analysis or batching enforcement. This represents a legal and compliance risk for hospital procurement.

- **Batching (temporal aggregation) is the standard recommended mitigation for small cell sizes.** The canonical protective approach from multiple regulatory frameworks (CMS PUF suppression, Manitoba PHIA protocol, California DHCS DDG) is to aggregate records until the group exceeds the minimum threshold rather than suppress entirely. For cliqueue, this translates to holding per-claim events off-chain and emitting a single `ClaimBatchSettled` event per provider per settlement cycle (e.g., daily or weekly) once the batch size exceeds `minBatchThreshold`. A configurable threshold of 11 (aligning with CMS standard) with an advisory recommendation of 20 (aligning with the "extremely high consequences" k-anonymity standard) is the defensible design.

**Design implication:** `ClaimsAdjudicator` should implement a configurable `minBatchThreshold` (default 11, advisory 20) per `providerHash`, holding individual claim state in contract storage but emitting only `ClaimBatchSettled(providerHash, batchSize, totalBilledCents, totalAdjudicatedCents, windowEnd)` events when the batch is flushed. Individual per-claim `ClaimSettled` events should be suppressed or emitted with zeroed amounts until the batch threshold is met. A `batchFlushInterval` (e.g., 7 days) ensures low-volume providers still settle on a regular cadence even if the count threshold is not reached. This design is consistent with the CMS n≥11 cell suppression standard and with k=20 for high-stakes deployments.
